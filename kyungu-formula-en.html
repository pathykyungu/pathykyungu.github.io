1. Introduction

The inverse Laplace transform plays a fundamental role in the analysis of dynamic systems, differential equations, and signal processing.

Kyungu‚Äôs formula offers a new and elegant approach to retrieve \( f(t) \) from \( F(p) \) without relying on complex integrals or classical tables.

2. Kyungu‚Äôs Formula (KL)

Given a Laplace function \( F(p) \), define an auxiliary function:

\[ \varphi(x) = F\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) \]

Then, the inverse Laplace transform \( f(t) \) is given by:

\[ f(t) = \varphi(0) \delta(t) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\varphi^{(n)}(0)}{(n!)^2} \cdot n \cdot t^{n-1} \]

The term \( \varphi(0) \delta(t) \) represents the impulsive component (when \( f \) contains a Dirac delta).

3. Example 1: \( F(p) = \frac{1}{p^2} \)

Here, \( \varphi(x) = x^2 \), so:

\[ \varphi^{(n)}(0) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } n \neq 2 \\ 2 & \text{if } n = 2 \end{cases} \Rightarrow f(t) = \frac{2}{(2!)^2} \cdot 2 \cdot t^{1} = t \]

This corresponds exactly to the inverse Laplace transform of \( \frac{1}{p^2} \).

4. Example 2: \( F(p) = \frac{1}{1 + p^3} \)

Applying Kyungu‚Äôs formula

Define:

\[ \varphi(x) = F\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) = \frac{x^3}{x^3 + 1} \]

Taylor series expansion:

\[ \varphi(x) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty (-1)^{k+1} x^{3k} \]

Thus, the derivatives are:

\[ \varphi^{(n)}(0) = \begin{cases} (-1)^{k+1} (3k)! & \text{if } n = 3k \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

And Kyungu‚Äôs formula gives:

\[ f(t) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{k+1} \cdot 3k}{(3k)!} \cdot t^{3k - 1} \]

Closed form recovered by real projection

Although the function \( F(p) = \dfrac{1}{1 + p^3} \) does not appear in classical tables, it is possible to recover a closed form from the complex sum by projecting it onto the real axis using the cube roots of unity.

Factorization:

\[ 1 + p^3 = (p + 1)(p + \omega)(p + \omega^2), \quad \omega = e^{2i\pi/3} \]

The inverse transform then becomes:

\[ f(t) = \frac{1}{3} e^{-t} + \frac{1}{3\omega^2} e^{-\omega t} + \frac{1}{3\omega} e^{-\omega^2 t} \]

Projecting this complex expression onto the real axis yields:

\[ \boxed{ f(t) = \frac{1}{3} e^{-t} + \frac{2}{3} e^{t/2} \cos\left( \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} t - \frac{\pi}{3} \right) } \]

This shows that the series obtained via Kyungu‚Äôs formula converges to this exact solution, even though it is absent from classical tables.

5. Conclusion

Kyungu‚Äôs formula provides a direct method to recover the inverse Laplace transform without tables or residues, by computing only a series derived from the auxiliary function \( \varphi(x) = F(1/x) \).

It also sheds light on closed forms even when they are missing from classical tables.
