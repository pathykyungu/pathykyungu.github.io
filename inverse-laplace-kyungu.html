🔁 What If We Started With the Inverse?

By Path Kyungu – Math educator and creator of the Kyungu formula
pathykyungu.github.io

1. What if we’ve been teaching everything in the wrong order?

In mathematics, we traditionally teach derivatives before integrals, direct transforms before inverse transforms, and definitions before real-world problems.

But what if that order is just a habit? What if understanding would come faster — and deeper — if we reversed the teaching sequence?

Here’s a radical example: 👉 What if we taught derivatives only after integrals?

Imagine a student first learning how to accumulate quantities — how to measure total effects, areas under curves, and long-term behaviors. Then, one day, the teacher introduces the derivative — not as a formal limit, but as a way to zoom in and retrieve the instantaneous from the accumulated.

Suddenly, the derivative would not be an abstract construct — it would feel necessary. It would be born out of intuition.

2. This is exactly what the Kyungu Formula proposes

Today, I propose a similar pedagogical inversion:

👉 Teach the inverse Laplace transform first — before the direct one.

In practice, whether in physics, engineering, or signal analysis, we are often given a function \( F(p) \) in the Laplace domain. Our real challenge is not to compute the Laplace transform, but to recover the original signal \( f(t) \) from \( F(p) \).

This is where the Kyungu–Laplace formula (KL) comes in:

\[ f(t) = \varphi(0)\delta(t) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\varphi^{(n)}(0)}{(n!)^2} \, n \, t^{n-1}, \quad \text{with} \quad \varphi(x) = F\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) \]

No complex integrals. No residues. Just an auxiliary function \( \varphi(x) \), its derivatives at zero, and a clean, computable series.

Even better — if we substitute \( t = -\ln x \), we obtain the inverse Mellin transform:

\[ \mathcal{M}^{-1}[F](x) = f(-\ln x) \]

This yields what I call the KML formula: Kyungu–Mellin–Laplace.

3. Why this reversal is powerful — and pedagogical

Teaching the inverse transform first:

  • Starts from a real-world problem (signal recovery)
  • Builds understanding through reconstruction, not abstraction
  • Makes the direct Laplace transform feel like a reversible tool, not an end in itself
  • Avoids heavy methods like contour integration or Laplace tables for tricky functions
  • Reveals deep connections between Laplace, Mellin, series, and impulse theory

And perhaps most importantly — it repositions the student not as a passive user of formulas, but as a mathematician searching for the origin.

4. So what comes next?

I truly believe that in the years to come, many teachers and students will naturally begin with inverse Laplace transforms — because that’s how we encounter problems in real life.

Just as teaching integrals first makes students see the big picture before zooming into the details, teaching the inverse first brings purpose and clarity.

"We used to teach the Laplace transform from left to right. But that was before we discovered how powerful the inverse could be."
