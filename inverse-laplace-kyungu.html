What If We Started With the Inverse?

By Path Kyungu
Contact: leprofesseurkyungu@gmail.com

1. Introduction

In traditional mathematics education, students learn derivatives before integrals, direct transforms before inverse transforms, and theory before applications. But is this sequence natural? What if a reversed approach led to deeper understanding?

This article proposes a new pedagogical framework inspired by the discovery of the Kyungu formula: start by teaching the inverse Laplace transform, and present the direct transform later, as a consequence.

2. The Kyungu Formula for Inverse Laplace Transform

The Kyungu formula allows the inversion of a Laplace-transformed function \( F(p) \) via a simple series, without resorting to complex contour integrals:

\[ f(t) = \varphi(0)\cdot \delta(t) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\varphi^{(n)}(0)}{(n!)^2} \cdot n \cdot t^{n-1} \]

with:

\[ \varphi(x) = F\left( \frac{1}{x} \right) \]

Here:

  • \( \delta(t) \) is the Dirac delta distribution (impulse at \( t=0 \))
  • \( \varphi^{(n)}(0) \) denotes the \(n\)-th derivative of the auxiliary function at zero

The inversion is thus performed purely using derivatives and powers of \( t \).

3. Application to the Inverse Mellin Transform

By introducing the logarithmic substitution \( t = -\ln(x) \), the formula extends to Mellin inversion:

\[ \mathcal{M}^{-1}[F](x) = \varphi(0)\cdot \delta(x-1) + \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\varphi^{(n)}(0)}{(n!)^2} \cdot n \cdot \left( \ln\frac{1}{x} \right)^{n-1} \]

This is called the Kyungu–Mellin–Laplace (KML) formula. The function is reconstructed directly from derivatives, avoiding classical integral representations.

4. Pedagogical Interest

The Kyungu approach invites educators to reverse the conventional teaching order:

  • Present the inverse Laplace transform first, based on real-world reconstruction problems
  • Introduce the direct Laplace transform as a tool derived from the inversion process
  • Make students work directly with signals \( f(t) \) and their recoveries, rather than abstract transforms
  • Emphasize analytic structures (series expansions, derivatives) over complex analysis

5. Philosophical Reflection

"We used to teach the Laplace transform from left to right. But that was before we discovered how powerful the inverse could be."

Starting with the inverse focuses the learner on reconstructing signals, finding causes from effects, and understanding transformations as reversible processes.

6. Conclusion

The Kyungu formula offers not only a mathematical tool but a new pedagogical paradigm. It transforms the inverse Laplace and Mellin transforms into simple, constructive processes based on series, accessible even without advanced integration techniques.

Perhaps it's time to teach inverses first.
